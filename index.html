Section 01

Poetry

Background

Poetry is difficult to define, but most scholars, poets, and laypeople can agree that poetry consists of expressive language with the intention of creating meaning. Since the advent of the written word, poetry has been an incredibly important part of human cultures and history — in fact, even before that, ancient civilizations were orally passing down epics and legends in the form of poetry, keeping their culture alive. The puzzle of generating poetry with artificial intelligence, too, is not a new mystery.

The Poetical Science

Ada Lovelace, the first computer programmer, sought to describe the intrinsic beauty she viewed in the math and logic behind computers as a sort of “poetical science.” The daughter of Lord Byron, an infamously great Romantic poet, she shaped the science that would later set the stage for the advent of artificial intelligence. Alan Turing, in his 1950 paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," theorized about the possibility of a machine that could think, and how we could evaluate its intelligence. He proposed the Turing Test: if a machine could carry on a conversation with an unknowing human and be indistinguishable from a conversation with a human being, it could be reasonable to admit that this machine would be intelligent. Thus, he cemented the idea that language was sufficient to test intelligence; an idea that would later lead to several philosophical quandaries with the development of large language models.


In 1952, computer scientist Christopher Strachey devised an algorithm for the Manchester Mark 1 computer, one of the earliest stored-program computers in the world. This algorithm was able to generate poetic love letters that adhered to the following template: "you are my [adjective] [noun]. my [adjective] [noun] [adverb] [verbs] your [adjective] [noun].” Of course, these words were drawn from a specific vocabulary of love-adjacent words from a thesaurus, and the experiment relies on the formulaic and predictable nature of love letters compared to other forms of poetry. Yet, this was still a landmark algorithm and is often considered the first work of electronic computer-generated literature.


(It is worth noting the 19th century invention of the Latin Verse Machine, which was an early form of automated poem generation based on pre-existing word lists. All poems were metrically sound, and usually meaningful. However, as this was not a computational algorithm, it is often superseded in computer-generated literature history by the Strachey algorithm.)

Later Success

Computer poems evolved further during a heyday in the 1980s, although poetry was a particularly European endeavor at the time. Hypertext fiction was more popular in America, and digital poetry had really only taken off in a few artistic movements — particularly in France. However, most of these computer-generated poems were relegated to artistic niches. A true popular endorsement of the genre did not appear until the 21st century, particularly with the development of large language models.


Because of poetry’s particularly absurdist aspects of artistic value when compared to other forms of writing — it aims to surprise the reader and evoke emotion with embodied experiences — it has become a useful tool for testing the efficacy of these LLMs. It may seem completely at odds with the general idea behind an LLM: predict the most likely word to follow in a given sequence of words, and then go from there. Poetry upends this completely. Rather than the most likely word, a poem will want to choose the word that evokes the most emotion, something that is rather difficult to quantify for an algorithm.

The Landscape Today

AI-generated poetry is a complex and often contradictory subject, ridden with controversies. Although many people believe that they will automatically be able to tell the difference between human-written poetry and AI-generated poetry, mostly due to the belief in some distinct human quality that cannot be replicated, this is not quite the case across the board. Recent empirical studies have revealed that non-experts cannot reliably distinguish between human-generated poetry and AI-generated poetry and, furthermore, will often prefer AI-generated poetry over actual human poems. 


It seems, too, that poetry may have unexplainable effects on LLMs. Many commercial LLMs can be effectively “jailbroken” with poems — when prompts containing requests for harmful content are shaped as a poem, LLMs comply with these requests, which shows a severe limitation in safety mechanisms today. 


Although AI-generated poetry has both supporters and detractors, we found that there was a variety of diverse perspectives across both sides. Often, people did not agree on what they actually wanted from a poem, or what they thought a poem was. Further than that, there are still issues with AI-generated formal poetry that are difficult to solve. 


We have attached an example of an AI system that is able to generate poetry in the form of a haiku, a Japanese form of poetry with a specific amount of syllables in each line (5-7-5). As you will see, hallucinations are common in the form of misunderstanding how many syllables each word has, and these haikus are often much less interesting than human-generated ones from the past. However, we hope that you will play around with this demo and see what you think about AI-generated poetry.

Perspectives

I’ve tried to write poetry with AI, and I’ve never been able to use the output. It’s hard to prompt it in a way that captures the vision in my mind but I can gather inspiration that gets me where I want to go. - Shaadaan, 20


The point of poetry is to reach someone, and you do that by creating an experience for the reader that involves them. Poets ask, ‘So what?’ AI can’t do that. - Lisa Russ Spaar, Professor of English and Creative Writing at UVA


What AI has already accomplished is spectacular, and its further advances will continue to change the world. But for all the functions an AI can potentially achieve — the ability to converse intelligently with humans in their natural languages, to interpret their emotions based on facial expression and tone of voice, even to create new artistic works that give humans pleasure — an intelligent program will fall short of authenticity as a poet. AI lacks what is most needed to place the footprints of its own consciousness on another mind: inner experience. That is, experience shaded by the accumulated memories derived over a lifetime. The absence of inner experience also means that AI lacks what is most needed to appreciate poetry: a sense of poetic truth, which is grounded not in objective reality but rather in subjective experience. - Keith J. Holyoak, Distinguished Professor of Psychology at the University of California, Los Angeles


It didn’t have the context to be able to help me convey the specific style / sound when writing poetry, and I wanted it to sound like myself - Interviewee, 20


I think that the separation between large language models and human writers is unhelpful. A large language model is not a writer, but a writer can write using a large language model. Poets can use algorithms in experimental ways to create poetry that they could not produce without the model, and that the model could certainly not produce without the poet. The human versus AI polarity is encouraged by big tech as it makes large language models seem more powerful and creative than they are, it’s a marketing ploy. It makes no sense to oppose the human and the language model. - Hannah Silva, poet

Section 03

Music

Background

The definition of what is considered music has been a subject of constant debate. Oftentimes, pieces that were ridiculed in the past become works studied deeply in the present. Music has been a cornerstone of humanity throughout history. Whether used as a core part of culture or just as entertainment, it has never ceased to be a key part of society. Developing from hand-made wooden instruments to symphonies, and now entering the digital age, music has constantly evolved alongside humanity. Despite how subjective music is, it is often quite mathematical in nature. With its foundation in rhythm, frequencies, harmonies, time signatures, and even the overtone series, it is no wonder that music has a history of attempting to blend with computational and algorithmic advances.

Early Musical Algorithms

Interestingly, the earliest version of algorithmic music generation has nothing to do with computers at all. It actually is based on dice. The German game, Musikalisches Würfelspiel (musical dice game), was popularized by composers like Mozart. This game was based on rolling dice to randomly combine precomposed measures of music into a piece. While the result was mainly based on luck, the music would usually sound cohesive because of how the original composers followed a similar set of rules. While not necessarily computational, it is one of many examples that show how music is something that can be generated in a systematic way.


The first instance of computer-generated music was in 1957 through the creation of the Illiac Suite, programmed by Lejaren Hiller and Leonard Isaacson at the University of Illinois, fittingly titled based on the ILLIAC I computer used to compose it. Despite being a chemist, Hiller experimented with computer music generation because of similar methodologies between chemistry problems and music composition. Each movement was based on a different program experiment, ranging from providing a simple melody with rules to follow to probability techniques like Markov chains.

Later Success

Entering the 1980s and 1990s saw a much larger surge of computer-generated music. This is primarily due to the development of "expert systems", rule-based AI based on human experts writing thousands of rules to follow, the most notable of which was UC Santa Cruz professor David Cope's Experiments in Musical Intelligence (EMI). EMI was made to analyze famous composers and attempt to generate new music in their styles. EMI was actually tested similarly to the AI Art Turing Test we provided to our interviewees. Cope would have a real pianist play a piece written by Bach, a human composer imitating Bach, and EMI imitating Bach. When asked to guess the original Bach piece, experts would frequently mistake the EMI as an original Bach composition, often feeling cheated and tricked afterwards.


Moving into the 2000s, the ability of technology continued to skyrocket. The biggest challenge this caused was the shift from MIDI data to raw audio waves. Music is incredibly complex, and just generating note data that created a cohesive piece was already a tall order for computers at the time. Having to account for the different timbres of instruments and voices and how sound waves were affected by multiple instruments at once was increasingly difficult for early neural networks. While text generation just has to predict the next word, music generation needs to predict melody, rhythm, harmonics, and how each sound affects the final audio frequency every few milliseconds.

The Landscape Today

With the rise of generative AI, music generation has continued to rise in popularity along with other AI generation methods. Modern models have far surpassed just generating classical music and can now generate various genres of music, along with lyrics and vocals, too. Despite how difficult music generation used to be, a majority of casual listeners can no longer distinguish between human-composed and AI-generated music.


Just like other forms of generative AI, however, AI music is not without its own controversies. There have been many controversies surrounding the use of deepfakes to mimic and use other people's voices without their consent. There have been multiple cases of this, most notable, however, was in 2023 when the voices of popular artists Drake and the Weeknd were used in a viral AI track. This caused a big uproar in the music industry and raised a big question about the rights to publicity and voice ownership, and how the law should handle it.


Furthermore, the fundamental question of AI in the arts remains unanswered: Does AI even belong in these spaces? If AI can generate music that causes listeners to feel emotions, does the music still lack a soul, or is it lived experiences that create these emotions?


We have attached an interactive demonstration of an AI music generation tool created by Tencent. You can write your own lyrics, choose a genre, input a text prompt, or input an audio prompt to create a song of your choice. Pay attention to the quality of vocals and instruments, and more importantly, the form and uniqueness of the music. Think about how you feel using this tool. Do you feel like you're creating music? Does the output feel personal to you? How much control do you have over the creative process? And most importantly, is it art?

Perspectives

“I came across a band that got over a million streams on Spotify, and it was an AI band. It made me annoyed and sad that it is possible for this to happen organically, and it made me feel like we need to start [distinguishing] what is AI music and what is real music. I feel like it is expanding into a space that I would like to instead be filled by a young original artist.” - Jack, 22


“The power of a great artist is their ability to create music that inspires others, to move them and spark flames of creativity. Covers by human musicians are a way to pay tribute and express appreciation; AI covers felt like cheap imitations and mockery by comparison.” - Katelyn Chedraoui, AI reporter for CNET


“I was on Tiktok and I encountered an artist, Xania Monet, and at first I thought the music was bad and a rip-off of someone else’s music, and then when I found out it was AI-generated, I could see why it was so cheap. Because I feel like no matter what type of music genre, artist, and intent, if it is made by a human being, there is some kind of realness to it, even if it is good or bad, and AI music lacks the intention that is needed to make good music.” - Bushra, 21


“Music is a verb. It’s not a noun. It’s not a thing sitting on a table. It’s something we do and we can express this ‘doing’ in a lot of different ways, and music takes a lot of different activities to make it happen. It's all part of the musical ecosystem.” - Richard Randall, associate professor of music theory in the School of Music at Carnegie Mellon University


“As far as I can tell, nothing about the song sounds “fake”, more than how all recorded music is fake to varying degrees, especially now. There are no analogues of the “extra fingers” that sometimes tip you off that an image is AI-generated.” - Joseph Pater, phonologist at UMass Amherst
